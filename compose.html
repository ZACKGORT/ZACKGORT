The Making
Hand-lasted · welt-constructed · built to resoleable
The Atelier

Every pair begins as a pattern drawn by hand in our workshop in León, Mexico — a city that has been making shoes since the sixteenth century. We work with the same families who taught us.

The leather comes from four tanneries, each chosen for a specific quality: the Horween Chromexcel for its pull-up depth, the Wickett & Craig harness for structure, a French calf for dress work, and a Brazilian suede that ages like nothing else.

"We don't do seasons. We do iterations. Each new run is a refinement of what came before."

Why We Make Them
The long version · worth reading · about eight minutes
The Essay

There's a specific kind of grief that happens when a well-made thing is thrown away. We felt it enough times that we decided to do something about it.

Fast fashion's invasion of footwear happened slowly, then all at once. The same disruption that hit clothing — speed, cheapness, disposability — hit shoes harder, because shoes require more craft to make well. When the price points collapsed, the craft had to go first.

"A shoe resoled is a shoe that didn't end up in a landfill. Over a lifetime, that's a dozen pairs that didn't get made."

The Goodyear welt construction we use was developed in 1869. It hasn't been improved because it doesn't need to be. A strip of leather — the welt — connects the upper to the insole and the outsole. When the sole wears out, you remove the stitching, replace the sole, and restitch it. The upper is untouched. The break-in is already done.

We offer lifetime resoling. Not as a marketing promise. As a business model. When you resole, we see you again. We remember what you ordered. We ask how the leather is coming in. We talk about the walk.
